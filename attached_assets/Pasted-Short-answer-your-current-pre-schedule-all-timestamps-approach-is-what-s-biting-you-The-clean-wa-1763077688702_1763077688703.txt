Short answer: your current “pre-schedule all timestamps” approach is what’s biting you. The clean way is:
	•	Store when each step becomes eligible (enrolledAt + delay).
	•	Let the queue coordinator assign the actual send slots day-by-day under the 50/day cap.
	•	Stop computing far-future scheduledAt timestamps at enrollment.

That kills the 30-day bug, the recalculation nightmare, and most concurrency headaches.

I’ll answer your concrete questions, but I’m going to recommend that structural change as the main fix.

⸻

0. Recommended architecture change (before the micro-questions)

Right now you do this:
	•	At enrollment: compute exact scheduledAt for all 4 steps and insert into sequence_scheduled_sends.

Instead, do this:

Tables:
	•	sequence_recipients
	•	id
	•	sequence_id
	•	enrolled_at
	•	last_step_sent (0–4)
	•	last_sent_at (nullable)
	•	sequence_steps
	•	sequence_id
	•	step_number
	•	delay_days (0, 3, 8, 10)
	•	sequence_scheduled_sends
	•	id
	•	recipient_id
	•	step_number
	•	eligible_at  ← earliest time this step may be sent
	•	scheduled_at ← NULL until the coordinator assigns a real slot
	•	sent_at      ← NULL until actually sent

At enrollment:

For each recipient:
	•	Insert 4 rows in sequence_scheduled_sends with:
	•	eligible_at = enrolled_at + stepDelay
	•	scheduled_at = NULL
	•	sent_at = NULL

Queue coordinator job (runs hourly or whatever):
	1.	Compute how many emails this sender has already sent in last 24 hours (sent_at count).
	2.	Remaining capacity = 50 - used.
	3.	Select from sequence_scheduled_sends:

SELECT *
FROM sequence_scheduled_sends
WHERE eligible_at <= now()
  AND sent_at IS NULL
  AND scheduled_at IS NULL
ORDER BY eligible_at, id
LIMIT remaining_capacity;


	4.	For each selected row:
	•	Set scheduled_at = now() and send (or enqueue to worker).
	•	On success: set sent_at = now() and bump last_step_sent/last_sent_at on recipient.

This way:
	•	No global “queue tail” to juggle at enrollment.
	•	No 30-day cutoff issue.
	•	Changing delays is simple: recalc eligible_at for unsent rows and clear scheduled_at.

You’re turning the system from “eager scheduler” into “eligibility + daily allocator”. Much saner.

Now let’s hit your specific questions using that as the north star.

⸻

1. Performance: 700 recipients × 4 steps = 2,800 inserts

2,800 inserts is nothing for Postgres.

Still, do it batched and in a transaction for cleanliness:
	•	Use one transaction per bulk enrollment.
	•	Use a multi-row INSERT ... VALUES (...) , (...) , ... or a parameterized bulk insert from Node.

Sequential per-row await insert() will work at your scale, but:
	•	It’s slower.
	•	It’s more fragile if something fails in the middle.
	•	It complicates recovering from partial bulk operations.

So:
	•	Technically: sequential is fine.
	•	Practically: batch insert in one transaction is better and easy enough.

⸻

2. Queue tail tracking: where should globalQueueTail live?

If you adopt the eligibility + coordinator model above, you don’t need a global tail at enrollment at all.

The coordinator uses:
	•	eligible_at
	•	sent_at
	•	and the 50/day cap

to decide what to send next.

If you insist on pre-assigning scheduledAt at enrollment, then:
	•	Never trust a single in-memory globalQueueTail if you have:
	•	multiple Node instances, or
	•	other jobs touching the queue.

Safer pattern:
	•	Have a sender_state table:

sender_state
-----------
sender_id
queue_tail_at
updated_at


	•	When bulk enrolling:
	•	Start a transaction.
	•	SELECT queue_tail_at FROM sender_state FOR UPDATE.
	•	Use that as your initial queueTailTime.
	•	Generate all scheduledAt values deterministically.
	•	UPDATE sender_state SET queue_tail_at = <newTail> at the end.
	•	Commit.

That gives you atomicity. But again: you avoid all of this if you don’t bake scheduledAt up front.

⸻

3. 30-day pre-scheduling window: keep, change, or remove?

The whole “30 days from now” horizon is the source of the cascade bug.

Pros of keeping it:
	•	Limits how far into the future you commit to a schedule.
	•	Might be useful if you had insane volume and were worried about endlessly long queues.

Cons:
	•	Creates edge cases like you saw: R1 fills the horizon and everyone else gets truncated.
	•	Makes UX weird: “Why does this sequence stop at 30 days even though the steps say 90?”

For your use case (700 recipients, 4 steps, 50/day):
	•	Remove it and rely on:
	•	50/day cap
	•	eligible_at logic
	•	the coordinator to drip things out.

If you really want a “safety horizon”, make it a soft warning, not a hard cutoff:
	•	“You’re scheduling sends 90 days into the future. Are you sure?”
	•	Internally, you still let it schedule.

⸻

4. Bulk-enrolling 700 people with a 50/day limit

What should happen:
	•	Schedule everything and let the queue naturally build.

You’re modeling a human who says:
	•	“I can only send 50/day.
Here is a backlog of people.
Work through them in order, respecting their delays.”

The correct behavior:
	•	The queue gets long.
	•	People see an estimated completion date.
	•	No one is silently dropped.

Implementation advice:
	•	Schedule all eligibility rows (eligible_at) for all 700 × 4 = 2,800.
	•	The coordinator sends whatever is eligible now, up to 50/day.
	•	Others wait until eligible_at arrives + capacity exists.

UX niceties you can add:
	•	Show a small banner: “At 50/day, this sequence will complete in ≈ X days.”
	•	Maybe warn if backlog > N days.

But don’t cut off scheduling just because volume is large. That defeats the purpose of automation.

⸻

5. Recalculation when delays change [0,3,8,10] → [0,2,5,7]

With your current “hard pre-scheduled scheduledAt” approach, this is a nightmare:
	•	You must:
	•	Delete all future scheduled sends (unsent).
	•	Recompute new scheduledAts in FIFO order.
	•	Make sure you don’t trample on things already sent.

With the eligibility model:
	•	scheduled_at is just “when the coordinator actually sent it”.
	•	Only eligible_at depends on the delay.

When delays change:
	1.	Update sequence_steps with new delay values.
	2.	For each unsent step row:

UPDATE sequence_scheduled_sends s
SET eligible_at = r.enrolled_at + INTERVAL '1 day' * new_delay
FROM sequence_recipients r
JOIN sequence_steps step
  ON step.sequence_id = r.sequence_id
 AND step.step_number = s.step_number
WHERE s.sent_at IS NULL
  AND s.sequence_id = :sequenceId;


	3.	Clear any scheduled_at for unsent rows if you were pre-setting it.
	4.	The coordinator now uses the updated eligible_at values.

No re-looping over recipients in Node. One SQL UPDATE does it.

If you insist on the old “timestamp scheduling at enrollment” design, your current sequential approach is fine for your scale; it’s just unnecessary complexity you don’t need.

What I’d tell the Replit dev, in one paragraph

We’re switching from pre-scheduling exact send times to an eligibility-based model. For each recipient and step, we store eligible_at (enrolled_at + delay) and let the queue coordinator assign actual send times day-by-day under a hard cap of 50 emails per 24 hours from this mailbox. All emails—new and follow-up—share this single pool. The coordinator selects unsent rows where eligible_at <= now() and scheduled_at IS NULL, in FIFO order, up to the remaining capacity. Changing step delays just recalculates eligible_at for unsent rows; we never have to rebuild a giant timestamp schedule.
