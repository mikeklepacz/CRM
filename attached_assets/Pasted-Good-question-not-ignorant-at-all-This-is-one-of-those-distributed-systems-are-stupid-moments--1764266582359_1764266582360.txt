Good question, not ignorant at all. This is one of those “distributed systems are stupid” moments.

Short version:
The thing that dials Twilio and the thing that must be listening for Twilio’s audio stream are not the same “awake window”.

Let’s break it down in human terms.

⸻

1. Two completely separate conversations

There are two network flows here:
	1.	You → Twilio (REST API call)
	•	Your backend wakes up (Autoscale), runs call_dispatcher, does:
	•	“Hey Twilio, please call +1… and when they answer, start a media stream to wss://nmucrm.replit.app/media-stream”
	•	That’s a quick HTTP request.
	•	It finishes in a fraction of a second.
	•	From Replit’s point of view: “Cool, request handled, I can go back to sleep soon.”
	2.	Twilio → You (WebSocket media stream)
	•	Some seconds later:
	•	Customer’s phone rings.
	•	They pick up.
	•	Now Twilio’s media server tries to open a WebSocket to wss://nmucrm.replit.app/media-stream.
	•	This is a completely new incoming connection from the internet.
	•	If your container has already gone idle, Replit has to:
	•	Spin it back up
	•	Establish routing
	•	Initialize Node
	•	Start your voice-proxy WebSocket server
	•	Twilio will only wait a small amount of time for that handshake. If it doesn’t complete, Twilio shrugs and kills the call.

So: Outbound “I called Twilio” does not guarantee inbound “I’m currently listening for Twilio.”

⸻

2. Autoscale’s job is to shut you down when idle

Autoscale is optimized for:
“Handle HTTP traffic, then sleep when nobody is hitting you.”

From Replit’s perspective:
	•	It sees an HTTP call (/dispatch-call or similar).
	•	It spins up your app, handles the request.
	•	After a short idle period, it happily kills the container to save resources.

Autoscale has no idea that Twilio is going to call back in 5–15 seconds on a WebSocket.
It just sees “no traffic, bye.”

By the time Twilio comes knocking with the <Stream> connection, your app may already be:
	•	Cold
	•	Half-started
	•	Not yet listening on the WebSocket route

That’s why you see:
	•	Call created ✅
	•	Phone rings ✅
	•	Zero seconds duration ❌
	•	No logs in voice-proxy ❌

Twilio never successfully finished the WebSocket handshake with your app.

⸻

3. Why “always-on” fixes it

If the container is always running:
	•	Your WebSocket server is already bound to /media-stream.
	•	When Twilio connects:
	•	TCP handshake → TLS → WebSocket upgrade → event loop
	•	All happens inside Twilio’s timeout window.
	•	Your proxy is ready to:
	•	Receive audio from Twilio
	•	Forward to ElevenLabs
	•	Send audio back
	•	Inject your background noise

No cold start, no race.

⸻

4. Mental model

Think of it like this:
	•	REST call to Twilio = you sending a letter to a call center telling them:
“When the customer picks up, call me on this specific walkie-talkie channel.”
	•	WebSocket = that walkie-talkie channel.

Autoscale:
	•	Wakes your radio just long enough to send the letter.
	•	Then switches the radio off to save battery.
	•	Later, the call center tries to reach you on the walkie-talkie channel.
	•	Your radio is off. They give up.

Always-on:
	•	The radio is on all the time.
	•	When they call back, you hear them.
