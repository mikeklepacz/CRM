Why Option A is the right model for a single mailbox

1. Gmail measures total volume per 24h, not “type” of email.
A follow-up counts the same as a first email. There is zero deliverability exemption for follow-ups.

2. You get one bucket: 50/day.
Everything coming out of that account must fit into the bucket.

If you give follow-ups their own “unlimited” track, you will eventually hit a wall:
	•	Week 1: 50/day first emails
	•	Week 2: 50/day first emails + 35/day follow-ups
	•	Week 3: 50/day first emails + 70/day follow-ups
	•	Week 4: 50/day first emails + 100/day follow-ups
	•	Week 5: Gmail flags you as a bot or campaign sender

That’s the death spiral.

Option A prevents this.

3. FIFO is human-like.
Step 1 goes out → waits its 3 days → gets scheduled like everyone else → waits for a free slot in the same daily bucket → then gets sent.

This mirrors a real human workload:
“I send 50 max per day, whether they are new or replies.”

Google sees that pattern as safe.

⸻

Why Option B only works for Apollo

Apollo is built on parallel mailboxes, so the “unlimited follow-up” trick isn’t real. It’s a resource illusion.

If follow-ups exceed capacity, Apollo silently spreads them across:
	•	the next day
	•	or another mailbox
	•	or another domain
	•	or a fallback API key

Plus, Apollo will pause sequences automatically if your bounce/spam rate spikes.

None of this exists in your stack.

If you implement Option B on one mailbox, Gmail will rate-limit you, soft-block you, or mark you as suspicious.
